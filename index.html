A brief history

In 1908, Corning Glass Works started making Nonex, a thermally resistant “non-expansion glass,” for railroad signal lanterns and other industrial applications. This clear glass moved into the kitchen through the efforts of Corning employee Jesse Littleton. As the origin story goes, he brought a sawed-off battery jar home to his wife Bessie, and she used the shallow mold to bake a cake. Capitalizing on the fact that the domestic sphere could benefit from the glass’s durability as much as the industrial world, Corning had a hit on its hands. By 1915, it was selling Pyrex pie plates, casserole dishes, and bakeware to the housewives of America. And despite the fact that many people hang onto their Pyrex pieces for a lifetime, it’s still selling and making its way into homes.

Popular Collections

Butterprint Collection

The Butterprint pattern was released in 1957 and quickly grew in popularity due to the turquoise on white/ white on turquoise varaities. Butterprint was one of the first patterns debuted on the new 'Cinderella Bowls' which were a serires of nesting bowls.

Butterfly Gold

The Butterfly Gold was released as a PYREX Compatibles pattern for the Correlle Dinner Ware pattern of the same name. Some pieces can even be found with both imperial and metric measurements listed on the backstamp. It was first released in 1972.

Spring Blossom Green

The Spring Blossom Green was released in 1972 to 1979 and a second phase in 1979 to 1981. This is one of the longest running patterns and is also one of the most popular. A nickname for Spring Blossom Green is 'Crazy Daisey'
